 ABSTRACT
Purpose: To Identify and catalog the sustainable practices adopted in the Amazon Region. Sustainable development in the Amazon region is fundamental for the balance between human presence and the preservation of nature. Every productive chain that involves Amazon needs to avoid the degradation of the region.
Theoretical framework: The theoretical bases that supported the research were extracted from scientific articles indexed by SCOPUS, from books about sustainability practices in the Amazon.
Methods: We perform a Systematic Literature Review (SLR) using the PRISMA protocol. The study covered articles published in SCOPUS from 1989 to 2022. As an inclusion criterion, the articles should cover topics related to sustainable development practices in the Amazon region. The articles were cataloged by journal, study area, economic sector, and sustainability practices.
Results and conclusions: Researchers from Latin America, mainly from Brazil, are the ones who are most dedicated to studying sustainable practices and actions in the Amazon region. The studies indicate a connection between production activities and actions that seek and are concerned with environmental sustainability. The researcher's interest in the Brazilian Amazon forest was identified, with emphasis on studies of sustainable forest management, deforestation, the study of rubber, geographic information systems for better monitoring of the region to contribute to planning and management, and the study of environmental degradation driven by the deficiency of government management.
Research implications: The sustainable actions and practices characterized in the Amazon examined show what is being studied by the scientific community globally in the Amazon region and in which economic segments the practices of technological sustainability can be observed.
Originality/value: Expands understanding of sustainable actions and presents proposals and recommendations for studies up to 2050 in the Amazon.
Keywords: Sustainable, Rainforest, Amazon, Technology.

1 INTRODUCTION
Sustainability is a term that has become common in the most diverse environments, such as corporate executives, investors, politicians, social activists, academics, and workers, among others (Agustini & Giannetti, 2018). It is a term used to define actions and activities that address human needs without compromising future generations (Un, 2022).
Sustainable development arose in 1980 in the document World Conservation Strategy, produced by the International Union for the Conservation of Nature (IUNC) (Barbiere, 2020). From 1987 onwards, sustainable development began to be more widely disseminated by the World Commission on the Environment and Development (CMMAD), known as the Brundtland Commission, for the document Our Common Future, with concepts and proposals with vast worldwide repercussions.
Sachs (2008) considers that sustainable development obeys a double ethical imperative of solidarity with present and future generations and, above all, requires the clarification of criteria in the social, environmental, and economic viability dimensions.
In this direction, in the 2030 agenda, 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) were defined and approved by 193 countries, including Brazil. The SDGs form an integrated and

The aims of SDGs are Poverty eradication; Zero hunger and sustainable agriculture; Health and well-being; 4 – Quality education; Gender equality; Clean water and sanitation; Accessible and clean energy; Decent work and economic growth; Industry innovation and infrastructure; Reduction of inequalities; Sustainable cities and communities; Responsible consumption and production; Action against global climate change; life in water; Earth life; Peace, justice and effective institutions and partnerships and means of implementation (Barbiere, 2020).
Grejo and Lunkes (2022) found that sustainability maturity contributes to the SDGs and that organizations can align their management practices with the SDGs by directing their business towards sustainability issues as companies end up contributing to the rational use of sustainable resources while generating economic development.
The Amazon Basin contains the largest contiguous tropical forest in the world, covers much of northwestern Brazil, and extends into Venezuela, Colombia, Peru, Guyana, Bolivia, Ecuador, and Paraguay, with an area of approximately 700 miles (46). Therefore, the importance of its biodiversity goes beyond Brazilian borders. The Amazon rainforest plays a valuable role in the global flow of carbon and the water cycle (50); tropical forests play vital roles in contributing to biodiversity, carbon cycles, hydrology, and climate patterns (Salisbury & Schmink, 2007).
From the sustainability perspective in the Amazon, the most discussed topics are the expansion of the agricultural frontier, food security, the introduction of biofuels, and the need for socio-environmental services as development progress (Martorano et al., 2016).
Projections of the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations indicate that the demand for food will continue to grow due to the need to meet the production of human and animal food, estimated at 3 billion tons by 2050 (FAO, 2009). In this sense, the central issue reconciles different objectives involving economic development and environmental sustainability, which often proves necessary tradeoffs (Perz, 2004).
Given this context, this study aimed to identify and catalog sustainable practices, impacts in the region, technologies developed, and areas of supply channels adopted in the Amazon Region. The articles dealt with topics related to sustainability practices in the Amazon region.
2 METHODS
This Systematic Literature Review was developed based on the PRISMA protocol (Mohe, Shamseer et al., 2016) to answer the research question: What sustainable practices are adopted in the Amazon Region?
The study's relevance is that tropical forests play a vital role in contributing to biodiversity, carbon cycles, hydrology, and climate patterns (Salisbury & Schmink, 2007) and are the global cornerstone of biological diversity (Peres, et al., 2016).
The preservation of forests has returned to the global agenda with the severe consequences of imminent climate change. Forest conversion contributes 17% to global CO2 emissions, and the world's forest stocks store more carbon than is contained in the atmosphere. Amazon region, which is suffering from severe deforestation, is the global community's focus because measures to halt the destructive dynamics of land use can significantly reduce future carbon emissions and protect biodiversity (IPCC, 2022).
In this context, the 2022 Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) shows the protection, restoration, and sustainable management of carbon-rich ecosystems (such as forests and peatlands). Together with the reduction of Greenhouse Gases (GHG) in food production, the containment of waste and switching to more sustainable diets are relatively low-cost measures that can mitigate the reduction of global warming by 2050 (IPCC, 2022).

2.1 Eligibility Criteria
The Systematic Literature Review process took place through a search in the SCOPUS database, applying the inclusion criteria. The choice of the database was due to the recognized insertion in the theme, maximizing the possibility of returning essential articles on the theme that answered the research question.
Only articles from journals in English and there was no time range limitation, and the articles should present sustainable practices in the Amazon.
As exclusion criteria, we set Literature Review articles, discussion on environmental conservation policies, and articles that came in a language other than English.
2.2 Search Strategy, Selection Process, Data Collection Process, and Synthesis Methods
Articles were from journals indexed in the SCOPUS database. The research took place in August 2022 through a qualitative and quantitative approach.
The selection first took place using the inclusion and exclusion criteria, and two researchers from August to October 2022, working independently at all stages, carried out the cataloging.
In the first phase, for selecting articles after reading the title and abstract, the pairs used the START program (Fabbr, et al., 2016). Then, after reading the introduction and conclusion, it was performed using a tool Excel spreadsheet to catalog the individual results as accepted or rejected after reading and analysis.
The analysis included identifying the main findings to systematically categorize the content of the articles and identify relationships about what is being discussed in the field of sustainable practices in the Amazon.
In the second phase, 70 articles were analyzed and selected by reviewers, and after reading the introduction and conclusion, 14 articles were selected. In the third phase was performed a complete reading of the 14 articles. Figure 1 demonstrates how the systematic review process took place.

In the third selected article, the authors explored the study of the cultivation of crops such as soy, corn, oil palm, and others introduced in anthropogenically modified areas in the Amazon Basin to supply the world's demand for food and energy. A successful experiment integrating the production of soybean and paricá (Glycine max L. and Schizolobium amazon com) based on previous research indicated potential top climatic zones for planting paricá in the Brazilian state of Pará (Martorano, et al., 2016).
The research showed that a no-tillage system reduces the effects of drought concerning the conventional planting still used by many farmers in the region. The study points to an economic segment of great importance worldwide: agriculture (Martorano, et al., 2016).

3.4.6 Agroforestry
The case study was performed in ecotone forests north of the Brazilian Amazon to demonstrate how forest management can become more sustainable through silvicultural restrictions that reduce forest degradation and soil erosion.
The effects of sustainable forest management (SFM) on tree diversity, wood volumes, and carbon stocks were evaluated. A continuous forest inventory was carried out in nine permanent plots of 1 ha (900 subplots of 100 m2), distributed in three treatments with three replications: T1 = control without selective cutting, T2 = SFM, and T3 = SFM + silvicultural treatment of thinning release by ringing and poisoning. The SFM was performed with an average of three commercial timber trees harvested per hectare with DBH (diameter at breast height) ≥50 cm, corresponding to a wood volume of 20±6m3ha-1, equivalent to the removal of 7±2 Mg C ha-1 (5% of original C) (Conde, et al., 2022).
The formation of clearings (gaps) resulting from the fall of exploited trees (0.12±0.09 ha) resulted in more significant impacts on the forest structure than the construction of 4 m skidder trails (0.06±0.04 ha), with an average of 7±4 damaged trees ha-1 and 5±3 dead trees ha-1. Although six tree species became locally extinct, tree diversity was little changed, and wood volumes and forest carbon stocks remained essentially constant at this average logging intensity. Based on the results, sustainable forest management can become an efficient land use model in the Amazon when harvesting is carried out with this average cutting intensity (Conde, et al., 2022).
Participatory Fuzzy Cognitive Mapping (FCM) was implemented to examine stakeholder perceptions regarding the sustainable development of two agroforestry frontier areas in the Bolivian and Brazilian Amazon. A range of development scenarios was explored and applied to the FCM derived from stakeholders, with climate change also analyzed (Blanco- Gutiérrez, et al., 2020).
Stakeholders in Brazil and Bolivia regions perceived landscapes of socio-economic impoverishment and environmental degradation driven by governmental and institutional deficiencies. Under such abject conditions, well-integrated governance and social and technological strategies offered socio-economic development, environmental conservation, and recovery (Blanco-Gutiérrez, et al., 2020).
In this study, the hypothesis was tested that the biogeochemical parameters of litter and soil serve as indicators of sustainable exploitation, as forest regeneration, C stocks, and C- nutrient ratios in soil and litter become progressively similar to those of primary forests as time passes after harvesting. They used a chronosequence spanning nine years since cutting to relate litter and soil (at 0–10, 10–30, 30–50cm depth) C stocks and 12 and 15 biogeochemical parameters, respectively, as well as canopy cover and tree density seedlings (10-150 cm tall) in evergreen Amazonian forests. In a new stand and four villages sampled three, five, seven, and nine years after logging, we compared 15 permanent plots (three 0.5 ha plots replicated by time category since logging).
Litter parameters were more sensitive to logging than soil parameters, as litter C stocks and C-nutrient ratios increased systematically after logging. Canopy cover decreased over time and was ~14% lower nine years after cutting. Total seedling density did not change consistently over time but was ~54% higher seven years after cutting. Our data suggest that the SFMP guidelines have served the purpose of maintaining soil quality and forest regeneration. Litter and soil parameters can be helpful indicators of sustainable forest management in upland evergreen forests in Central Amazonia (Bomfim, et al., 2020).

The firewood value index classified the residues of D. excelsa wood as the most promising for bioenergy (Lima, Castro, Baptista, & Silva, 2020).
The study consisted of presenting the process of re-signification of rubber in forest areas in the Amazon as a way of building sustainability scenarios in the Amazon and discussing, through this experience, how design strategies can contribute to optimizing processes of innovation and social development, especially in Conservation (Sarmento & Moura, 2017).
The first stage of the study presents reflections on design and its connections and interrelationships with the Amazonian context. The second analyzes the transformations detected in the Amazon today, the aspects related to sustainability and local development, and their repercussions in the forestry context. The third stage focuses on the use of rubber by local communities as a concrete example of the challenges and opportunities related to design based on two distinct strategies: the first, the analysis of different aspects of latex extraction, its characteristics and history, and the importance of rubber in the Amazonian socio-environmental context (Sarmento & Moura, 2017).
The study tried to highlight a transition in environmental governance in the Brazilian Amazon due to the need for a new pattern of socio-economic development. A sub-municipal analysis of socio-economic development and deforestation in the Brazilian Amazon was carried out from 2000-2010 (Tritsch & Arvor, 2016).
The sustainability practices consist of applying the soil clearing method, combined or not with fallow enriched with legumes, which is a promising alternative technique, as the retention of organic residues in clearing can promote soil fertility. The study's objective was to evaluate the effect of tillage without fire on the physicochemical properties of the soil, comparing the effects of traditional slash and burn with: slash and mulch with enriched fallow in cultivated areas and slash and burn without enriched fallow in pastures. Cutting and covering a fallow enriched with legumes conserved soil density and significantly increased nutrient concentrations and organic matter content compared to the burned cultivated area and control forest (Comte, et al., 2012).
The emergence of Brazilian extractive reserves reinforced the notion that the sustainable use of the forest could play a central role in conservation. Brazil nuts are considered a model non-timber product for promoting conservation through use. Demographic studies, however, have demonstrated differential impacts of nut harvesting on nut population structure.
Thus, comparing three populations with different histories of forest use, degrees of road access, and current levels of nut harvesting, the following question was asked: are they showing regeneration failure? Are seedling/sprout densities explained by the adult Brazil nut's structure, the fruits' fate, and the forest's general structure?
Four plots of nine ha were installed in each location to census chestnut trees P10 cm DBH to carry out the study. Within each plot, 36 subplots (25 · 25 m each) to quantify regeneration, the general structure of the forest, and fruit counts open and closed. Approximately 29-55% of the fruits were not harvested, and dispersers opened more than 90%. These practical initiatives resulted in many locally developed management plans and were accompanied by real gains in local Brazil nut prices. These initiatives that link ecologically correct management with economic gains at the local level can serve as an example for other protected areas in which local communities are vital actors in the conservation or conversion of tropical forests (Wadt, Kainer, Staudhammer, & Serrano, 2008).

4 CONCLUSION
The observed articles answered the central research question, allowing the identification and cataloging of sustainable practices adopted in the Amazon Region. Most of the studies are by researchers from Latin American countries, mainly from Brazil. It also explains why Brazilians are at the forefront of Amazonian studies. There is a concentration of study areas in the states of Amazonas, Acre, Pará, Roraima, and Mato Grosso in Brazil. In other words, researchers from the Brazilian Amazon regions are concerned about carrying out studies that aim to understand sustainable actions in the location. These are significant findings of this work, along with the actions found.
The 14 selected articles identified sustainable actions and practices characterized in the Amazon. It was possible to map an article in the following economic segments: environmental governance, conservation market, agriculture, entrepreneurship, and sustainable tourism, and nine articles in the agroforestry segment. Identified the interest of the researchers through the Brazilian Amazon forest, studies of sustainable forest management, deforestation, a study of rubber, geographic information systems for better monitoring of the region to contribute to planning and management, and study of environmental degradation driven by the deficiency of government management.
Using only a single database was a limitation of the work, as it could have used another international database, such as the JCR. The suggestion for future work is to expand the databases and use a regional base, especially in Latin America, since 68% of the researchers come from Latin American countries. Still, as proposals and recommendations to have a more sustainable Amazon by 2050, further studies are suggested aimed at the agribusiness and agroforestry economic segments, mainly because they can be a source of foment for the development of public policies for sustainable development.
