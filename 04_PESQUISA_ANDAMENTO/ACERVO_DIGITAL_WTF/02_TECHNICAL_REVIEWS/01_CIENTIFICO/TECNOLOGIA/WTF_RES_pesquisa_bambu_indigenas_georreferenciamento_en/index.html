    Mapping unmanaged forests reveals bamboo should not be seen as a hindrance to timber management but as an asset that increases forest resilience and productivity (EMBRAPA, 2018). Integrating remote sensing data with Territorial and Environmental Management Plans (PGTAs) for the Nukini, Puyanawa, and Nawa indigenous lands can transform these territories into models for productive conservation (CPI-ACRE, 2020).

    In the far west of the state, the Upper Juruá concentration includes ethnicities like the Nukini, Nawa, Puyanawa, and Katukina (CLÍNICA DHDA, 2024). These communities reside in territories bordering the Serra do Divisor National Park, a high-biodiversity area where submontane forest mixes with bamboo patches (SEMA-AC, 2025).

    Territorial management is guided by Territorial and Environmental Management Plans (PGTAs), which use ethnomapping to identify available natural resources and plan their use (SOUSA, 2019). For the Nukini people, for example, ethnomapping is a vital tool for management and conservation, allowing the community to decide how and where to collect bamboo and other forest products without compromising sustainability (CPI-ACRE, 2020).

    AAFIs represent a unique category of local specialists joining traditional knowledge with modern management techniques. They are responsible for implementing agroforestry systems and monitoring nurseries and management areas (SOUSA, 2019). Josias Maná Huni Kuĩ, from the Kaxinawá Indigenous Land of the Rio Jordão, is an example of an AAFI acting as an indigenous consultant, assisting in articulation between communities and bodies like UFAC and CPI-Acre (CPI-ACRE, 2024).

    Bamboo exploration in Acre is governed by a regulatory framework seeking to balance economic development with environmental protection (LEGISWEB, 2017). The Brazilian Forest Code (Law No. 12,651/2012) already opens exceptions for non-timber forest products, facilitating bamboo management.

    IMAC Normative Instruction No. 10/2015

    IMAC's IN 10/2015 establishes the reference term for environmental licensing of bamboo management. The main points of this norm include:

    1. Environmental Impact Study Requirement: Recruitment of multidisciplinary teams registered with IMAC is necessary (ESTADO DO ACRE; IMAC, 2015).
    2. Sustainable Management Plans: Exploration must follow a plan ensuring natural regeneration (IBAMA, 2015).
    3. Monitoring and Tracking: Use of systems like SINAFLOR to monitor origin and transport of stems (IBAMA, 2015).

    Recently, in 2025, IMAC reinforced licensing requirements, aiming to increase fiscalizing rigor of environmental interventions in the state (AC24HORAS, 2025).

    Future Perspectives and Conclusions

    Acre state is in a privileged position to lead the bamboo bioeconomy in the Amazon. The vast natural reserve of the Guadua genus, combined with a history of sustainable development policies, creates a favorable environment for large-scale projects. However, success depends on maintaining articulation between UFAC, IFAC, and IMAC and effectively including indigenous communities at all stages.
